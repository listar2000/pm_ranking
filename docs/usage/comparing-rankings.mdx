`pm_rank` exposes two rank-correlation utilities for comparing the rankings
produced by different models. Both take two `Dict[str, int]` rankings and
return a single float in `[-1, 1]`.

```python
from pm_rank import spearman_correlation, kendall_correlation
```

## Why compare rankings?

Different ranking methods optimize different things:

- **Brier** rewards calibrated, sharp probabilistic forecasts.
- **Bradley-Terry** rewards being relatively better than your peers, even if
  everyone is poorly calibrated.
- **AverageReturn** rewards being better than the *market*, which is a
  different baseline again.
- **IRT** rewards getting hard problems right (as identified by the model).

If two methods strongly agree on a forecaster ranking, you can be confident
the ranking reflects underlying skill rather than artifacts of the metric.
If they disagree, that disagreement is itself informative.

## Worked example

```python
from pm_rank import (
    BrierScoringRule, GeneralizedBT,
    spearman_correlation, kendall_correlation,
)

# Fit two different models on the same problems
_, brier_rankings = BrierScoringRule().fit(problems)
_, bt_rankings    = GeneralizedBT().fit(problems)

print("Spearman:", spearman_correlation(brier_rankings, bt_rankings))
print("Kendall :", kendall_correlation(brier_rankings, bt_rankings))
```

```
Spearman: 1.0
Kendall : 1.0
```

In our [quick-start example](/quickstart) Brier and BT produce the *same*
ranking (Alice > Carol > Bob), so both correlations are 1. On real data the
two methods typically agree at the top of the leaderboard but diverge in the
middle.

## What the numbers mean

| Value | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| `1.0` | Perfectly identical ordering. |
| `0.0` | No relationship — the rankings are statistically independent. |
| `-1.0` | Perfectly reversed ordering. |

[Spearman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spearman%27s_rank_correlation_coefficient)
is the Pearson correlation of the rank values themselves. It's sensitive to
the magnitude of rank differences — moving from rank 1 to rank 10 hurts more
than moving from rank 1 to rank 2.

[Kendall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kendall_rank_correlation_coefficient)
counts the fraction of forecaster *pairs* that are ordered the same way in
both rankings, minus the fraction ordered oppositely. It's less sensitive to
single outliers and is generally more conservative.

<Tip>
**Rule of thumb:** if two methods give Spearman > 0.85 on a real dataset,
they're effectively measuring the same thing. If they give Spearman < 0.5,
they're measuring genuinely different aspects of forecaster skill, and
which one to trust depends on what question you're asking.
</Tip>
